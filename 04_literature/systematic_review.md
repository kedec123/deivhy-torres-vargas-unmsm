# Mini Systematic Review: Child Anemia in Peru

## 4.1. Review Question

What does Peru-focused literature show about the persistence, determinants, territorial inequalities, and intervention challenges related to anemia in children, especially those under 36 months of age?

## 4.2. Search Strategy

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Database | PubMed |
| Search date | June 19, 2026 |
| Search approach | Broad search first, then screening for policy and analytical relevance |

**Boolean search string**

```text
("anemia"[Title/Abstract] OR "anaemia"[Title/Abstract])
AND "Peru"[Title/Abstract]
AND ("child*"[Title/Abstract] OR "infant*"[Title/Abstract] OR "toddler*"[Title/Abstract])
```

The search returned **140 records**.

## 4.3. Screening Criteria

**Inclusion criteria**

- Peru-focused studies, or studies with a clearly usable Peruvian child sample
- Children under 5 years of age, with priority for studies centered on 6 to 35 or 6 to 59 months
- Anemia as a main outcome or a central analytical variable
- Studies on prevalence, determinants, inequalities, spatial distribution, supplementation, implementation, or program performance
- Peer-reviewed journal articles in English or Spanish

**Exclusion criteria**

- Adult-only or pregnancy-only studies
- No Peru-specific analytical contribution
- Anemia mentioned only marginally
- Protocols, editorials, or highly biomedical papers with little relevance for the present question
- Studies too narrow to support the intended national framing

## 4.5. Included Studies

| Study | Main contribution to this project |
|---|---|
| Sobrino et al. (2014) | Provides a national historical anchor and shows that child anemia remained socially patterned even when other nutrition indicators changed. |
| Velasquez-Hurtado et al. (2016) | Offers one of the clearest national analyses of factors associated with anemia in children under three years of age. |
| Accinelli and Leon-Abarca (2020) | Helps frame the role of altitude and the measurement debate that becomes especially important for recent comparisons. |
| Berky et al. (2020) | Examines government interventions and raises questions about why formal policy efforts do not automatically reduce the burden. |
| Brewer et al. (2020) | Shows that supplementation strategies can fail because daily implementation and caregiver uptake are not simple. |
| Dorsey et al. (2021) | Connects child, caregiver, and community factors, which helps keep the analysis from collapsing into a single-variable explanation. |
| Whitney et al. (2021) | Adds evidence from an intervention setting and shows that anemia reduction is possible, but not through one isolated action alone. |
| Al-kassab-Cordova et al. (2022) | Directly supports the territorial inequality angle by comparing rural and urban patterns. |
| Al-kassab-Cordova et al. (2023) | Strengthens the inequality argument through decomposition analysis and highlights the role of structural conditions. |
| Cerda-Hernandez et al. (2024) | Adds recent spatial evidence and reinforces the need to think beyond national averages. |

## 4.6. Main Synthesis

The literature points in a consistent direction. Child anemia in Peru is persistent, territorially uneven, and strongly tied to structural inequality. The burden is repeatedly higher among younger children, poorer households, rural populations, and high-burden territories in the sierra and selva.

The review also shows that the problem cannot be reduced to one explanation. Maternal education, living conditions, geography, access to preventive care, and implementation quality all matter. That is why purely descriptive reporting is not enough and why supplementation policy should not be read as self-executing.

Another clear pattern is that national averages often hide important spatial concentration. More recent studies increasingly rely on decomposition and spatial methods, which suggests that policy-relevant research now needs sharper attention to where the burden remains clustered.

## 4.7. What This Means for the Present Study

This review supports the direction of the current project, but it also clarifies its niche. Peru already has important studies on prevalence and determinants, yet there is still room for a focused 2019-2024 analysis that deals explicitly with persistence, inequality, and comparability under the updated guideline context. That is the practical value of the proposed study.
