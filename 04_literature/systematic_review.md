# Systematic Literature Review for Child Anemia Research

## 4.1. Research Question

What does recent Peru-focused literature show about the prevalence, inequalities, determinants, spatial distribution, and intervention challenges associated with anemia in children, especially those under 36 months of age?

## 4.2. Search Strategy

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Database** | PubMed |
| **Date of search** | June 19, 2026 |
| **Search scope** | No year restriction at search stage; final synthesis prioritized recent and policy-relevant literature |

**Boolean search string:**

("anemia"[Title/Abstract] OR "anaemia"[Title/Abstract])
AND "Peru"[Title/Abstract]
AND ("child*"[Title/Abstract] OR "infant*"[Title/Abstract] OR "toddler*"[Title/Abstract])

This search returned **140 records** in PubMed on June 19, 2026.

## 4.3. Inclusion & Exclusion Criteria

**Inclusion criteria (defined before screening):**

- Peru-focused studies or studies with a clearly identifiable Peruvian child subsample
- Children under 5 years of age, with priority given to studies centered on children 6 to 35 or 6 to 59 months
- Anemia as a primary outcome or a central analytical variable
- Studies on prevalence, determinants, inequalities, spatial distribution, supplementation, program evaluation, or implementation barriers
- Peer-reviewed journal articles in English or Spanish

**Exclusion criteria:**

- Studies focused on adults, pregnancy only, or non-child populations
- No Peru-specific analytical contribution
- No usable anemia outcome for the present research question
- Protocols, editorials, historical or archaeological analyses, or highly biomedical studies without population or policy relevance
- Studies where anemia appeared only as a peripheral variable without meaningful results for child health or inequality analysis

## 4.5. The 10 Selected Papers

1. **Sobrino, M., Gutierrez, C., Cunha, A. J., Davila, M., & Alarcon, J. (2014).** *Child malnutrition in children under 5 years of age in Peru: trends and determinants.* *Revista Panamericana de Salud Publica.*
   *Reason for inclusion: National ENDES-based anchor study showing that anemia declined historically but remained structurally high and socially patterned.*

2. **Velasquez-Hurtado, J. E., et al. (2016).** *Factors associated with anemia in children under three years of age in Peru: analysis of ENDES 2007-2013.* *Biomedica.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Strong national determinants study directly centered on children 6 to 35 months, highly aligned with the current project.*

3. **Accinelli, R. A., & Leon-Abarca, J. A. (2020).** *Age and altitude of residence determine anemia prevalence in Peruvian 6 to 35 months old children.* *PLOS One.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Important for the altitude-adjustment debate and for understanding how geography alters anemia estimates in Peru.*

4. **Berky, A. J., et al. (2020).** *Evaluation of Peruvian Government Interventions to Reduce Childhood Anemia.* *Annals of Global Health.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Connects the anemia burden to state interventions and raises questions about effectiveness beyond formal policy design.*

5. **Brewer, J. D., et al. (2020).** *Behavioral Insights Into Micronutrient Powder Use for Childhood Anemia in Arequipa, Peru.* *Global Health: Science and Practice.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Explains why supplementation strategies may fail in practice even when they exist in policy and service delivery.*

6. **Dorsey, A. F., et al. (2021).** *Child, caretaker, and community: Testing predictors of anemia and response to iron supplementation in Peruvian preschool-aged children.* *American Journal of Human Biology.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Multi-level view of predictors and supplementation response, useful for linking biological and social explanations.*

7. **Whitney, R., et al. (2021).** *Impact of a collaborative childhood anaemia intervention programme in Peru.* *Tropical Medicine & International Health.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Provides intervention evidence from a community-based multimodal program rather than from supplementation alone.*

8. **Al-kassab-Cordova, A. A., et al. (2022).** *Rural and urban disparities in anemia among Peruvian children aged 6-59 months: a multivariate decomposition and spatial analysis.* *Rural and Remote Health.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Directly relevant to territorial inequality and to the rural burden highlighted in ENDES 2024.*

9. **Al-kassab-Cordova, A. A., et al. (2023).** *Inequalities in anemia among Peruvian children aged 6-59 months: a decomposition analysis.* *Frontiers in Public Health.*
   *Reason for inclusion: Quantifies pro-poor inequality and identifies the contribution of maternal education, region, and household factors.*

10. **Cerda-Hernandez, J., Sikov, A., & Vidal-Valenzuela, L. Y. (2024).** *Analisis espacial de la anemia infantil en Peru 2022: construccion de mapas a nivel distrital para politicas publicas.* *Salud Publica de Mexico.*
    *Reason for inclusion: Offers district-level spatial targeting logic and shows clustering in high-burden territories such as Puno, Loreto, Ucayali, and Madre de Dios.*

## 4.6. Main Synthesis

Across the selected literature, five patterns appear consistently. First, anemia in Peru is persistent and unequally distributed rather than randomly dispersed. Second, the highest burden tends to concentrate among younger children, poorer households, rural settings, and territories in the sierra and selva. Third, maternal education, household living conditions, and access to preventive practices repeatedly appear as relevant explanatory variables. Fourth, supplementation and anti-anemia policies do not automatically translate into successful outcomes because implementation barriers, adherence, and local context matter. Fifth, spatial analysis is becoming increasingly important, suggesting that national averages alone may hide clusters of severe burden that require more precise targeting.

## 4.7. Interpretation for the Present Project

The literature strongly supports the direction of the present research protocol. It confirms that Peru already has enough evidence to treat child anemia as a structural inequality problem, but not yet enough harmonized analysis focused on the 2019-2024 period under the current comparability debate. That is where the present project can contribute: by combining recent ENDES-based trend analysis with a sharper focus on persistence, inequality, and the methodological challenge created by changing anemia definitions.
