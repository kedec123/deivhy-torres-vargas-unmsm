# Research Question and Method-Fit Matrix

## 2.1. Refined Research Question

To what extent did anemia prevalence among Peruvian children aged 6 to 35 months change between 2019 and 2024, and which maternal, household, and territorial factors were most strongly associated with its persistence in the harmonized ENDES series?

## 2.2. Three Candidate Methods

| Method | Short Description |
|---|---|
| **Method 1 - Repeated cross-sectional secondary data analysis** | Use ENDES 2019-2024 microdata to estimate trends and model associations between child anemia and structural variables such as residence, region, wealth, maternal education, and recent supplementation. |
| **Method 2 - Primary cross-sectional field survey** | Collect original data in one or two high-burden territories to measure anemia and related risk factors directly, with stronger local detail but weaker national reach. |
| **Method 3 - Quasi-experimental program evaluation** | Estimate the effect of an intervention, such as supplementation or nutrition support, using a non-randomized policy evaluation strategy. |

## 2.3. E.D.F.C.V. Matrix

| Criterion | What it asks | Method 1 | Method 2 | Method 3 |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| **E - Epistemological fit** | Does the method match the quantitative paradigm? | 5 | 4 | 5 |
| **D - Data availability** | Can the required data be accessed realistically? | 5 | 2 | 2 |
| **F - Feasibility** | Can it be done well within the present course stage? | 5 | 2 | 2 |
| **C - Contribution type** | Does it answer the actual question being asked? | 5 | 3 | 3 |
| **V - Venue fit** | Does it fit likely public health and nutrition venues? | 5 | 4 | 4 |
| **Total** |  | **25** | **15** | **16** |

The matrix supports the choice, but it does not replace judgment. The point is to defend the method, not just to score it.

## 2.4. Why Method 1 Wins

Method 1 is the best fit because the question is national in scope and explicitly asks about change over time plus associated factors. ENDES is already designed for that type of population-level analysis. It gives enough coverage, repeated measurement, and policy relevance to build a solid descriptive and analytical baseline.

This method also respects the sequence of the project. Before asking whether a specific intervention caused a reduction, I first need to establish where anemia remains concentrated and which inequalities continue to matter. That is the kind of work repeated cross-sectional analysis does well.

## 2.5. Why Method 2 Does Not Win

A primary field survey would offer richer local detail, especially in departments with a severe burden, but it would narrow a question that is currently national. It would also introduce much heavier field logistics, ethics procedures, and operational costs than the present stage of the project can reasonably support.

In other words, it could become a valuable later extension, but it is not the smartest first move for this research question.

## 2.6. Why Method 3 Does Not Win

A quasi-experimental design is appealing because it sounds closer to policy impact. The problem is that it demands stronger assumptions, better intervention exposure data, and a much clearer identification strategy than I have at this stage. If I forced that design too early, the project would sound more causal than the available evidence can honestly support.

## 2.7. Open Tension

The chosen method is strong for trend description and association modeling, but weaker for causal explanation. If the project later evolves into a policy evaluation study, it may need to shift toward a quasi-experimental design. For now, though, the defensible choice is to build a reliable national baseline first.
