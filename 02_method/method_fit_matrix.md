# Research Question + Method-Fit Matrix

## 2.1. Research Question (Method-Ready Precision)

To what extent did anemia prevalence among Peruvian children aged 6 to 35 months change between 2019 and 2024, and which maternal, household, and territorial factors were most strongly associated with that persistence, based on harmonized ENDES data collected under nationally standardized criteria?

## 2.2. Three Candidate Methods

| Method | Method 1 - Repeated Cross-Sectional Secondary Data Analysis | Method 2 - Primary Cross-Sectional Field Survey | Method 3 - Quasi-Experimental Program Evaluation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Description** | Analyze ENDES 2019-2024 microdata to estimate national trends and model associations between anemia and variables such as maternal education, wealth, residence, natural region, and iron supplementation. Output: nationally grounded estimates and statistical association models. | Collect original data in selected high-burden departments such as Puno or Loreto to measure anemia and household risk factors directly. Output: richer local context, but limited national comparability. | Evaluate the causal effect of a public intervention such as iron supplementation or nutrition programs using quasi-experimental strategies. Output: policy effect estimates if identification is credible. |

## 2.4. Defense of the Winner: Method 1 - Repeated Cross-Sectional Secondary Data Analysis

**Why Method 1 wins:**

The current research question is fundamentally about two things: change over time and patterned association. It asks how anemia levels evolved between 2019 and 2024 and which measurable inequalities are most strongly linked to its persistence. That is exactly the kind of question best answered through repeated cross-sectional analysis of a nationally standardized survey such as ENDES. This method provides immediate access to a large, policy-relevant dataset, allows subgroup comparisons across years, and supports inferential models that remain fully consistent with the positivist paradigm already chosen in Session 1.

Method 1 is also the strongest option because it respects the scale of the problem. Child anemia in Peru is not only a local phenomenon; it is a national public health issue with strong territorial and socioeconomic variation. ENDES offers national coverage, repeated measurement, and variables directly aligned with the study question. That combination makes it possible to produce evidence that is both analytically defensible and useful for policy discussion.

**Why not Method 2 (Primary Field Survey):**

A primary field survey could generate richer contextual detail in one or two high-burden departments, but it would not be the best first method for this question. It would reduce national comparability, require substantial logistics, ethical approvals, training, travel, and possibly biomarker collection, all of which are disproportionate for the current stage of the project. More importantly, it would narrow the scope of a question that is explicitly national.

**Why not Method 3 (Quasi-Experimental Evaluation):**

A quasi-experimental design sounds attractive because it moves closer to causal claims, but the current project is not yet in a strong position to support that move. To estimate the effect of supplementation or nutrition programs credibly, I would need clearer intervention exposure data, stronger assumptions about comparability, and a defensible identification strategy. At this stage, forcing a causal design would create more methodological risk than clarity.

**One open tension:**

The strongest method for the current question is excellent for describing trends and estimating associations, but it is weaker for establishing causality. If the project later shifts toward evaluating which policies actually reduce anemia, the methodological center of gravity may need to move toward quasi-experimental designs. For now, however, the smarter decision is to first build a robust national baseline.
