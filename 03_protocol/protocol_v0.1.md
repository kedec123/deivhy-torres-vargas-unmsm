# Research Protocol Outline (v0.1)

## 3.1. Title

Persistence of Child Anemia in Peru: A Repeated Cross-Sectional Analysis of Inequalities Among Children Aged 6 to 35 Months, 2019-2024.

## 3.2. Abstract

Child anemia remains one of the most persistent public health challenges in Peru, especially during the first three years of life, when nutritional deprivation can affect cognitive, motor, and social development. Using harmonized ENDES data for the period 2019-2024, this study examines how anemia prevalence evolved among children aged 6 to 35 months and which maternal, household, and territorial factors were most strongly associated with its persistence. The study adopts a quantitative empirical approach and uses repeated cross-sectional secondary data analysis based on nationally standardized survey microdata. The expected contribution is a clearer national understanding of patterned inequality in child anemia, with evidence relevant for nutrition policy, child health planning, and the implementation of the 2024-2030 multisectoral plan against maternal and child anemia in Peru.

## 3.3. Introduction & Problem Statement

### Problem Statement

Child anemia in Peru remains at a level that is too high to be treated as a residual problem. According to the harmonized ENDES 2019-2024 series under the updated WHO 2024 and RM 251-2024-MINSA criteria, anemia among children aged 6 to 35 months rose from 31.4% in 2019 to 35.3% in 2024. In the same 2024 measurement, the burden was higher in rural areas (44.7%) than in urban areas (31.2%), with especially high levels in departments such as Puno (53.1%) and Loreto (50.9%). These figures show that anemia is not randomly distributed; it follows territorial and socioeconomic inequalities that remain insufficiently explained in an integrated national analysis.

Although Peru has implemented multiple nutrition and supplementation strategies, the persistence of anemia suggests that the problem cannot be understood only as a matter of individual diet. It is also shaped by poverty, maternal education, access to preventive care, rural disadvantage, and unequal living conditions. A stronger evidence base is needed to clarify how these factors relate to anemia persistence over time and which inequalities should be prioritized by public policy.

### Relevance - The "So What?" Test

This research matters because anemia in early childhood is linked to impaired cognitive and motor development, weaker learning conditions, and longer-term social disadvantage. At the policy level, the problem is urgent enough that Peru approved a multisectoral national plan for 2024-2030 specifically aimed at reducing maternal and child anemia. A robust analysis of national trends and associated inequalities can help move the discussion beyond generic concern and toward better targeted interventions.

This project is also relevant because it addresses a concrete policy question with real national implications: if anemia remains persistently high despite years of intervention, where is the burden concentrated and which factors are most consistently associated with it? Answering that question is useful not only for academic purposes, but also for ministries, regional governments, and child development programs that need evidence to prioritize action.

## 3.4. Brief Literature and Policy Background

The public health literature consistently treats anemia in early childhood as a serious developmental risk rather than a minor nutritional fluctuation. WHO notes that anemia in severe cases can affect cognitive and motor development in children, while Peruvian institutional sources and UNICEF materials emphasize its implications for early learning, language, and social development. In Peru, the policy relevance of the topic has intensified in recent years, especially with the approval of the national multisectoral plan for 2024-2030.

At the national level, ENDES provides the most important empirical basis for monitoring child anemia because it allows repeated measurement with large-scale coverage and policy-relevant disaggregation. However, descriptive reporting alone does not fully explain persistence. What is still needed is an integrated analytical account of how anemia varies over time and how strongly it is associated with maternal, household, and territorial inequalities across the national sample.

## 3.5. Research Questions / Hypotheses

### General Research Question

To what extent did anemia prevalence among Peruvian children aged 6 to 35 months change between 2019 and 2024, and which maternal, household, and territorial factors were most strongly associated with its persistence?

### Specific Research Questions

- How did anemia prevalence among children aged 6 to 35 months evolve in Peru between 2019 and 2024 at the national level?
- How does anemia prevalence differ by area of residence, natural region, wealth quintile, and maternal education?
- Which child, maternal, household, and territorial variables show the strongest statistical association with anemia in the pooled 2019-2024 dataset?
- To what extent does recent iron supplementation appear associated with lower anemia prevalence after controlling for key sociodemographic variables?

### Working Hypothesis

Child anemia in Peru remained persistently high between 2019 and 2024 and was more strongly associated with structural inequality than with isolated individual factors. More specifically, anemia is expected to be more frequent among children living in rural areas, in poorer households, in the sierra and selva, and among those whose mothers have lower educational attainment or weaker access to preventive health measures such as iron supplementation.

## 3.6. Methodology

### Research Paradigm

Quantitative empirical (positivist) paradigm.

### Study Design

Repeated cross-sectional secondary data analysis using ENDES microdata for the years 2019 to 2024.

### Data Source

- **Primary source:** Encuesta Demografica y de Salud Familiar (ENDES), 2019-2024.
- **Population of interest:** Children aged 6 to 35 months in Peru.
- **Analytical frame:** Nationally harmonized anemia estimates and associated sociodemographic variables available in the survey microdata.

### Main Variables

- **Outcome variable:** Anemia status in children aged 6 to 35 months, based on hemoglobin measurement and the harmonized analytical criteria used for the selected series.
- **Child-level variables:** Age in months, sex, recent iron supplementation, and other available child health variables.
- **Maternal variables:** Educational attainment, age, and other relevant maternal characteristics available in ENDES.
- **Household and territorial variables:** Area of residence, natural region, department, wealth quintile, and other living-condition indicators available in the survey.

### Analytical Strategy

The study will proceed in four stages:

1. Descriptive analysis of anemia prevalence from 2019 to 2024 at the national level.
2. Comparative analysis across key subgroups such as rural/urban residence, natural region, wealth quintile, and maternal education.
3. Multivariable modeling using survey-weighted logistic regression to estimate which factors are most strongly associated with anemia in the pooled dataset.
4. Sensitivity checks on comparability, especially regarding the 2024 guideline update and how the harmonized series is operationalized in the final analytical dataset.

Whenever appropriate, the analysis will use the complex survey design features of ENDES, including weights and relevant stratification variables, to avoid biased national inference.

### Expected Statistical Tools

- Descriptive trend tables and figures
- Cross-tabulations by subgroup
- Survey-weighted logistic regression
- Possible pooled models with year effects and selected interaction terms

## 3.7. Ethical Considerations

This research relies on secondary analysis of anonymized survey microdata and does not involve direct contact with children, caregivers, or health personnel. For that reason, the ethical risk is low. Even so, the study requires careful interpretation because territorial disparities can easily be reported in a stigmatizing way. Departments or rural populations with higher anemia prevalence should not be framed as culturally deficient, but as groups facing unequal structural conditions.

The project will also avoid overstating causal claims. Associations identified in repeated cross-sectional data will be presented honestly as associations unless a stronger identification strategy is later introduced.

## 3.8. Expected Results

- A clear national trend profile of child anemia in Peru between 2019 and 2024.
- A disaggregated picture of inequality by residence, natural region, maternal education, and wealth.
- A multivariable model identifying the factors most strongly associated with anemia persistence.
- A research-based discussion of how current policy efforts could be better targeted.
- A protocol and analytical foundation that can later support a publishable manuscript or a more advanced causal study.
