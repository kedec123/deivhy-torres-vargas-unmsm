# Paradigm Justification Statement

## 1.1. Research Topic and Context

Child anemia remains one of the most persistent public health problems in Peru, especially among children aged 6 to 35 months. Based on the nationally comparable ENDES 2019-2024 series reported under the updated WHO 2024 and RM 251-2024-MINSA criteria, anemia in this age group increased from 31.4% in 2019 to 35.3% in 2024. The burden is not evenly distributed: in 2024, prevalence was higher in rural areas (44.7%) than in urban areas (31.2%), and departments such as Puno (53.1%) and Loreto (50.9%) showed especially high levels. This suggests that anemia in Peru is not only a nutritional issue, but also a territorial, social, and health system problem.

My tentative topic focuses on understanding how child anemia has evolved in Peru during the 2019-2024 period and which maternal, household, and territorial factors are associated with its persistence. I am using 2019-2024 because ENDES 2024 already presents a comparable national series for those years under the updated anemia guideline, which makes this period methodologically cleaner than mixing older and newer definitions without adjustment.

## 1.2. Preliminary Research Question

How have anemia levels among Peruvian children aged 6 to 35 months changed between 2019 and 2024, and which maternal, household, and territorial factors are most strongly associated with the persistence of high anemia prevalence?

An alternative, more policy-oriented version would be:

To what extent do social, maternal, and territorial inequalities help explain the persistence of child anemia in Peru between 2019 and 2024?

## 1.3. Chosen Paradigm and Justification

This study is best framed within a **quantitative empirical (positivist) paradigm**. The core reason is simple: the phenomenon under study is defined through measurable health indicators, nationally standardized survey instruments, and observable differences across time, territory, and socioeconomic conditions. The main task is to estimate patterns, compare groups, and test associations using structured data rather than to interpret lived meanings or co-design an artifact.

I do **not** choose a purely interpretivist paradigm because, although interviews could later help explain caregiver practices or barriers to iron supplementation, that approach would not answer the first research need: to quantify the magnitude, distribution, and associated factors of anemia at the national level. I also do **not** choose a computational paradigm as the primary one, because building predictive models before establishing a clear descriptive and analytical baseline would be premature. A computational extension could make sense later, but not as the starting point of this study. I similarly avoid mixed methods as the primary framing for this first stage, because the current question is still mainly centered on measurable prevalence and associated factors from secondary survey data.

The positivist choice fits the logic of the project: anemia is treated here as a measurable population-level condition, and the study seeks evidence through statistical regularities rather than through subjective interpretation. In other words, the research problem asks first for robust measurement and explanation of patterned inequality.

## 1.4. Implications of the Paradigm Choice

**Data type:** Repeated cross-sectional quantitative data, mainly from ENDES 2019-2024, with emphasis on hemoglobin-based anemia status in children aged 6 to 35 months and associated variables such as area of residence, natural region, wealth quintile, maternal education, access to supplementation, and other child or household characteristics available in the survey.

**Methods:** Secondary data analysis, descriptive trend analysis, subgroup comparison, and inferential statistical models such as logistic regression or multilevel modeling, depending on the final structure of the dataset and the research scope.

**Expected contribution:** A clearer, evidence-based understanding of how child anemia has evolved in Peru in recent years and which structural inequalities are most strongly linked to its persistence. This contribution would be more useful for public health and nutrition policy than a generic statement that anemia is still a problem.

**Natural academic space:** Public health, child nutrition, epidemiology, and health policy research.

## 1.5. One Doubt or Tension

My main methodological tension is comparability across years. In 2024, Peru adopted the updated WHO guideline through RM 251-2024-MINSA, which changed the altitude adjustment formula and the cutoff for some age groups. That means I need to be careful not to mix older anemia figures and updated figures as if they were identical. A defensible strategy is to use the harmonized 2019-2024 series reported in ENDES 2024, but I still need to verify whether the microdata I use for deeper analysis are fully comparable under the same measurement framework.
